The Buddhist doctrine that there is no permanent, unchanging, independent self or soul (attā) underlying existence. What we call the 'self' is a conventional label for a constantly changing bundle of five aggregates (skandhas): form, feeling, perception, mental formations, and consciousness. None of these is permanent. None exists independently. None constitutes an enduring 'me.' The sense of being a unified, continuous self is a constructed experience — useful for navigating the world but ultimately empty of inherent existence. Realizing anattā is not losing oneself; it is losing the illusion of a fixed self and thereby gaining freedom from the suffering that arises from clinging to it.

Śūnyatā (Emptiness)

The Buddhist philosophical principle that all phenomena are empty of inherent, independent existence. Things do not possess an essential, unchanging nature or 'self' (svabhāva). Rather, everything arises in dependence on causes and conditions, exists...

Click to expand ↓

The Buddhist philosophical principle that all phenomena are empty of inherent, independent existence. Things do not possess an essential, unchanging nature or 'self' (svabhāva). Rather, everything arises in dependence on causes and conditions, exists relationally, and lacks any permanent core. Emptiness is not nothingness — it is the recognition that things exist conventionally while being ultimately empty of independent essence. The Heart Sutra crystallizes this: 'Form is emptiness, emptiness is form.' To understand emptiness is to see through the illusion of solidity without falling into nihilism. It is the middle way between eternalism (things permanently exist) and annihilationism (nothing exists at all).
